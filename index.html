01 — Lithosphere

The
Crust

Surface layer

Earth's outermost shell: a rigid mosaic of silicate rock ranging from 5 km beneath the ocean floor to 70 km beneath the highest mountain ranges. It is the stage upon which all terrestrial life unfolds — yet it makes up barely 1% of Earth's total volume.

70km Max depth
~1% Earth volume
02 — Mesosphere

The
Mantle

Silicate rock layer

A vast ocean of solid rock, nearly 2,900 km thick. Though rigid, the mantle flows over geological timescales through convection — slow currents of heat that push tectonic plates, build mountains, and swallow ocean floors back into the deep.

2,900 km thick
84% Earth volume
03 — Outer Core

Liquid
Iron

Metallic fluid layer

A churning sea of liquid iron and nickel at temperatures exceeding 5,000°C. Its turbulent convection — coupled with Earth's rotation — generates the geodynamo: the magnetic field that shields life from solar wind and would-be extinction.

5,000° Celsius
2,260 km thick
04 — Inner Core

Solid
Heart

Iron-nickel alloy

A sphere of solid iron the size of the Moon, compressed by 3.5 million atmospheres of pressure into a crystalline lattice. It rotates slightly faster than the planet above — completing an extra revolution every few centuries in quiet, unknowable solitude.

1,220 km radius
6,000° Celsius
05 — Atmosphere

The
Veil

Five distinct layers

A thin membrane of gas — 78% nitrogen, 21% oxygen — held by gravity alone. What we call "sky" is merely 12 km of troposphere. Beyond lies strata of decreasing pressure, fading gradually into the vacuum of space.

Troposphere — 0–12 km
Stratosphere — 12–50 km
Mesosphere — 50–85 km
Thermosphere — 85–600 km
Exosphere — 600–10,000 km
